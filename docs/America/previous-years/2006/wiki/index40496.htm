Why do this talk?

Every year at the town hall we ask how many are at their first YAPC. The number is consistently around 50%. So, although some of this stuff seems like common sense, there are always a few new people around.

Why are you here?

  • Getting your money's worth
  • The Brandt Thirds

When attending sessions, try to strike some sort of balance. You can't possibly spend 100% of your time being 100% useful for your company. At the same time, don't 100% check out–you won't be coming to more conferences.

Consider the "Thirds Rule" to be fair to yourself.

    • 1/3 stuff you want to learn ("should" do these for work/projects)
    • 1/3 stuff you think is interesting ("want" to attend these for fun/interest factor)
    • 1/3 stuff you don't know anything about (no idea what these are about)
      • Is this a good technology, or are they bozos?
  • Reporting back to the mothership
    • Let your company know it was worth it to send you
      • Set a goal, like writing down 5 relevant points you can go over with your boss when you get back. Make sure they are relevant to work.
    • Pass on contacts to tech and business people

Before you fire up your laptop

This is the "There be Dragons" warning if you are using your laptop in a wide-open wireless environment for the first time. All of your stuff is flying through the air, and at a tech conference there are plenty of people who know how to intercept it. Take a few precautions when you're using the wireless networks:

  • If you are checking email, use some secure protocol to secure your username/password and data. Common options are using ssh to get to a box where you can read email or using a VPN to get a secure network before you fire up your email client.
  • I saw someone in a session with a laptop privacy screen and these seem like a great idea. It prevents casual observers from seeing what you are working on. You can check out 3Ms website for details.

Meeting the Big Egos

  • Pro Etiquette
    • Speaking as someone who might be considered a "big name", I have to say I think most of the advice on that page seems inappropriate to me. Maybe some of it makes sense at a really enormous conference, but for YAPC it is overkill. I'd suggest that better advice for YAPC is to just try to be as normal and polite to Big Names as to anyone else. If you saw someone you didn't know in a restaurant, would you just sit down with them? No. But if you were attending the same conference, you might ask if you could join them. Fine. What's the proper place for book signings? I can't speak for anyone else, but I'd be honored to be asked to sign a book just about any time. – Dominus
    • A lot of it doesn't apply specifically to YAPC, but even at small conferences and workshops I've had people try to engage me in long discussions when I'm trying to set up my laptop with the projector three minutes before a talk, or when I'm going into the restroom, or when there is also a long line of people waiting to also ask me something. Most of the advice in "Pro Etiquette" is aimed at ComicCon attendees where there isn't a community so much as an audience, but the overall message is still good: be considerate of the situation.
